# Cuttability Rubric

Cuttable content is text that sounds confident but doesn't add meaning — filler words, redundant structures, and entire sentences that set up rather than contribute. David's voice rejects padding. These sections flag the most common cuttable patterns: adverbs that qualify rather than strengthen, phrases that can be contracted, and sentences that announce themselves instead of landing.

## Filler adverbs

Words that soften claims without adding specificity:

- really
- very
- quite
- basically
- literally
- actually
- obviously
- essentially

These adverbs sound emphatic but undercut the main verb. "It was really important" → "It was important." The claim stands stronger without them.

## Redundant clauses

Phrases that repeat in different words or say in two what one word says:

- "which is" — unnecessary relative clause
- "that is to say" — adds nothing to what precedes it
- "in order to" → "to" — the goal is clear from context
- "at this point in time" → "now" — padding instead of precision
- "the reason why is" — use a colon or restructure
- "as a matter of fact" — conversational hedge masquerading as emphasis

These structures are safe but verbose. Cut them.

## Entire sentences that restate or set up

Sentences that announce themselves instead of moving forward:

- "Let me explain."
- "Here's the thing:"
- "Before I begin,"
- "To summarize,"
- "In conclusion,"
- "So here's what I mean:"
- "This is important:"
- "The point is,"

These are frame sentences — they tell the reader what's coming instead of just arriving. The paragraph stands stronger without them.
