# Benchmark: limerick

Two drafts of the same constraint. Limericks are the sharpest test of voice because the form is rigid: a slack rhyme or a slipped foot shows up immediately. An LLM asked for a limerick usually produces a limerick-shaped object that isn't funny — this file exists to make the difference obvious.

## The constraint

Write a five-line limerick. Strict AABBA rhyme. 8-8-5-5-8 meter (anapestic). Subject: **writing with an AI that keeps rewriting your voice.** The joke should land without being explained.

## In-voice draft (good)

I asked Claude to write me an email.
It helped. It refined. It detailed.
    I said, "Looks like me."
    It said, "Yes — and free."
I rewrote every word. Claude prevailed.

## AI-slop draft (bad)

There once was a writer who tried
To make his own voice, but then sighed
    The AI did well,
    But the words felt like shells,
And he realized that voice was his pride.

## What to look for

Formal constraints like limericks are the sharpest test of voice. A voice profile that produces a limerick with a weak A rhyme, a flat punchline, or — worst — a final line that explains the joke is a profile that doesn't know its user's sense of humor.

Specifically: the in-voice draft lands "Claude prevailed" as the punchline because the reader has to do the work — the joke is that even the act of rewriting has already been shaped by the AI's suggestion, so the human has already lost. The AI-slop draft hits "words felt like shells" as a slant rhyme (shells/well, off by a consonant), then the fifth line ("voice was his pride") explains the moral instead of letting the setup do its work. The in-voice draft trusts the reader; the slop draft hand-holds them.
